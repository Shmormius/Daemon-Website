No Longer Human

by Osamu Dazai

Pages: 176

Genre: Fiction

Read: 1/2/2024

This is the first book I read in 2024, it is also what inspired me to try reading more in 2024. The presentation and premise of this book is very interesting, especially with the history of the author in mind. The main character is so misrible at times I almost struggle to feel bad for him. This is a book I can't stop thinking about and I find it really fascinating how it exposed the authors gross feelings on life and other people.

Kokoro

by Natsume Souseki

Pages: 256

Genre: Fiction

Read: 1/5/2024

I really loved this book, it left me very sad for a couple days after finishing it. What I did was search "books if you liked no longer human" and this was overwhelmingly the result. I can see why, discussion of suicide, authors final work, parts of the story told in letter form, similar time period, and more. Overall the book does a good job of making you think about what it is that you are living for and what that means for the people around you.

House of Leaves

by Mark Z. Danielewski

Pages: 736

Genre: Novel, Horror fiction, Ergodic literature

Read: 1/21/2024

Truth be told, I am not much of a reader. This project was made to encourage me to read more and so far has worked towards that goal. However, this book was a huge test. Starting with relatively short books to build my attention span and reading speed this is the first rather long book I have tried to read. 736 is much more than ~200, on top of its nature as "ergodic literature," this was extremely hard to read. However I did enjoy it, it's a genuinely very finely crafted and unsettling story with a metric ton of deeper meaning laced into every page. Although at times the unconventional way the book is structured, reading compiled transcription of a book written about a movie all layers of which have their own horror, works to take away some of the genuine horror aspects. I found this book because I wanted more "nightmare-like" horror, that is to say, horror that feels like the disjointed and nonsensical feeling one has in bad dreams. To that end this book delivers. It also gives me the impression that this book partly inspired the internet-driven obsession with "liminal spaces" particularly in the beginning when the subject and director of the movie about which this book is written discovers the strange things about their house. So overall I enjoyed this but I have trouble recommending it because of how much effort is involved in reading it.

Fire Punch Vol.1

by Tatsuki Fujimoto

Pages: 200

Genre: Dark fantasy, Dystopian, Supernatural

Read: 1/21/2024

This is another one of Fujimoto's works, one that I had not gotten around to reading until now. I had thought that maybe since this is one of his earlier works it would be lacking the nuance he shows in his current work. However, after seeing online discourse around this series suggest that is has a lot of deeper meaning and themes I was persuaded to try it. I can certainly see the set-up being constructed to delve into these deeper themes but unfortunately the book doesnt quite dive into them fully, so thats a hope for volume 2. I can tell a huge theme in this is going to be about movies, similar to the themes in House of Leaves, but probably channeled through Fujimoto's passion for cinema. In all I am excited to see where this goes, the art was really good as usual and Fujimoto's creativity needs to be admired.

Chainsawman Vol.14

by Tatsuki Fujimoto

Pages: 192

Genre: Dark fantasy, Comedy Horror

Read: 2/6/2024

I debated for a while whether or not to include books like these where I already deep into the series but if I didn't I would only have one book for February lol. Chainsawman is kind of one of my guilty pleasures because not only does it seem like mindless action schlock media on the surface but if you take it for face value it kind of is. However I think it is actually a masterclass in character and depth if you actually take the time to read it with an open mind, because I thought that when I bought it in 2022 because I was sure it would be goofy. As funny as it sounds to have this be one of only three 5 star ratings so far, I legitimately could not justify giving it a lower rating, especially considering this is my own list and its based on personal enjoyment. Seeing the two main characters of the series interact seriously for the first time since the start of 'part two'(volume 12-Ongoing) and having room for conncections back to the trauma experienced in part one in a subtle way while developing new characters and stakes is done really well. Not to mention character design and art being good as well, I enjoyed it like I normally do this authors works, especially when you dont pay attention to the type of people the series generally attracts on the internet lol.

Fire Punch Vol.2

by Tatsuki Fujimoto

Pages: 208

Genre: Dark fantasy, Dystopian, Supernatural

Read: 2/26/2024

I am SWAMPED with school right now, I had predicted that I would see a loss in reading through-put due to the school year beginning again but I didnt think this bad. This may be the one of the only books I finish in Febuary which is a bit sad. I am currently reading Natsume Soeseki's 'I am a Cat' and a short story collection of HP Lovecraft at a snails pace. It was kind of nice being able to have the satisfaction of finishing a book. Either way this volume was definitely good in the same way that the previous one was. However I am not loving the shock value throughout this one. It can be said that all of Fujimoto's work has shock value, but until this book I hadn't really considered what made his other examples of this good and what was icky about this one. One of the big things was that there was very little value, character-wise or thematically from it. Like I can easily see justifications for why he would want to flesh out the dystopian world in that way, but unfortunately I think it crosses into the gradtuidous. While I will be reading the next one(whenever I see it at a store I guess) I wouldn't even recommend the series as a whole. I will hold out my overall judgement until he gets a chance to pay off some of this thematic build-up that the story is working toward though.

Quartet: Intermediate Japanese Across the Four Language Skills Vol. 1

by Akemi Yasui, Yuriko Ide, Miyuki Doi, Hideki Hamada

Pages: 259

Genre: Textbook, Reference Work

Read: 3/6/2024

I was in class today and we officially finished this textbook and it occured to me: "does this count?" Like where do I draw the line? Originally this part of the blog started as a way for me to keep track of novels and the like. But then I added a reference work and other non-traditional books now I am muddled. Either way I decided that since I have spent considerable amounts of time reading this book this year, it's definitely worth recording it. Like "Hey why did I not read much in February?" and this is one of the examples why. Either way I actually liked this textbook. Our previous textbooks were the infamous Genki I and Genki II, and those were good. But something about those books felt juvinile. It felt very good to move to a textbook where the info was less in english and was able to assume that you knew the basics already.
